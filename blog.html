Legislation · April 24, 2026 · 9 min read

What Maine LD 307 actually did — and why every state is copying it.

By the editors

The 20 MW threshold in Maine's data-center moratorium is the most consequential number written into US energy policy in a decade. Most coverage has missed why.

On April 7, 2026, Maine's House passed LD 307 by 82-62, the Senate by 19-13, and the bill became the first US statewide data-center moratorium. Through November 2027, no new data center over 20 MW can break ground in the state. Smaller builds — 20 MW and under — must clear a new siting review that includes municipal veto power, a cooling-water study, grid-impact analysis, and explicit consideration of microgrid alternatives.

Most national press played this as a Maine story. It is not a Maine story. It is a template, and at last count six other states have either introduced or moved bills using substantially the same statutory language: South Carolina (H 5526 and H 5286), Wisconsin (AB 1099 and SB 1061), Oklahoma (SB 1488), Georgia (HB 1059), Virginia (HB 1515), and Maryland (HB 120). All seven bills are currently in committee. The pattern is too tight to be coincidence — it's coordinated lifting of the Maine model.

The 20 MW number is the whole point

The threshold sits surgically below hyperscaler economics. Microsoft, Meta, Google, AWS, Oracle, and the OpenAI/Stargate joint-venture builds all start at 50-200 MW per phase, with full-build campuses landing in the 500-1500 MW range. Anything under 20 MW is sub-scale: small AI training clusters, edge-compute deployments, colocation pods. None of those are what the people protesting in Festus, Independence, Loudoun, and Cumberland County are actually mad about.

The number is calibrated. Higher and the bill looks like it's protecting incumbents while excluding small operators. Lower and it bans every server room in the state. 20 MW threads the needle: the bill reads as moderate ("we're not banning data centers, just hyperscale ones") while functionally producing a hyperscale-only ban. That's why it passed bipartisan, and that's why six other state caucuses have copied the language verbatim.

The cleanest explanation came from a Reddit comment that's been cited in three subsequent state hearings: "Maine's 20 MW line shows the real bottleneck is power, not chips. Every state with a Maine-style bill in committee is conceding the chips are coming — but they don't have to land here."

The microgrid clause is the second weapon

Buried in section 4(b) of LD 307 is a requirement that any sub-20-MW project must demonstrate it has "considered and documented microgrid, behind-the-meter, and co-located generation alternatives" before approval. This is more important than it sounds.

For a hyperscaler, the appeal of a Maine-sized state has always been straightforward grid interconnection at industrial rates. Microgrid alternatives mean a meaningfully more complex stack: on-site combined-cycle gas, battery storage, possibly nuclear SMRs, all permitted separately, all subject to their own community opposition. The microgrid clause doesn't say "you must use microgrids" — it says "you must prove you considered them and explain why you didn't." In land-use law, that's a permanent slowing mechanism, not a speed bump.

Both the South Carolina and Wisconsin copycat bills include identical language. That's the template propagating.

What developers should read from this

Three things. First: Maine is functionally off-limits for hyperscale through November 2027 at minimum. Plan accordingly. Second: the contagion is faster than press coverage captures. CT, GA, MD, NJ, SC, VA, and WI all have Maine-template bills already in committee, with sponsor counts rising. By the time these are in The Wall Street Journal, the legislative calendar has already moved against you. Third: the 20 MW number will become the de facto national review threshold. States that haven't passed moratoriums will still start treating 20 MW+ as the line where local opposition activates.

For your siting team, that means a candidate-list strategy: maintain active land options in at least two states with no Maine-template legislation in committee, and monitor weekly for movement. The states currently without any restrictive bill in committee — Texas, North Carolina, Iowa, Minnesota, Ohio, Indiana, Tennessee, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, Idaho, Wyoming, the Dakotas — are not guaranteed to stay clean. A single co-sponsor in any of those statehouses is the leading indicator.

What activists should read from this

Two things. First: nine months of organizing produced LD 307. The bill's lead sponsor has been clear that the Festus-Missouri recall and the Loudoun by-right elimination were what convinced moderate-Republican co-sponsors that data-center opposition was bipartisan, not coastal-progressive. The Maine model only worked because the broader environment proved the politics. Second: microgrids are the unexpected on-ramp. Anti-AI framing did not move centrists. Anti-fossil-grid-buildout framing did. Local groups in OK, GA, and SC are already pivoting their public messaging to lead with grid impact rather than community impact, with measurable improvement in both press uptake and legislator engagement.

The lesson, in both directions, is that LD 307 is not the ceiling. It's the floor.

Maine took nine months to pass a 20 MW number. Oklahoma City took 30 days to say "us too." The activation cost just collapsed.

What we'll be tracking next

The seven copycat bills currently in committee carry an aggregate sponsor count of 84 legislators across both parties. We'll publish weekly updates in The Sunday Watch on which of those bills pick up new co-sponsors, which move out of committee, and which add Maine-style microgrid clauses. The first of those committee-vote windows opens in late May. Subscribe to follow the cascade.

Is Dominion Energy doing Northern Virginia wrong?

By the editors

Loudoun County eliminated by-right data-center approval in March. The VA Court of Appeals froze the Digital Gateway in April. Dominion is still pricing $15 billion in transmission as if neither happened. That's either contrarian conviction, or the most expensive form of denial in US utility planning.

Northern Virginia hosts somewhere between 25% and 35% of global enterprise data-center capacity, depending on how you count. The cluster centered on Loudoun County is so dense that more intercontinental internet traffic flows through Loudoun than through any other county on Earth. Dominion Energy, Virginia's monopoly utility, has spent the past four years building the transmission backbone to serve another decade of that growth — over $15B in approved or pending T&D projects, nearly all of it backstopped by data-center load forecasts.

Two things have happened in the last 60 days that should make every line of that forecast suspect.

March 15: Loudoun pulled the rug

Loudoun County's Board of Supervisors eliminated by-right data-center approval on March 15. Until that date, any project meeting basic zoning requirements could be permitted administratively, without a public hearing or board vote. Now, every new project requires a Special Exception — a discretionary approval process with public hearings, neighbor notification, and a vote subject to political winds.

This is, structurally, the single largest regulatory change ever imposed on the data-center industry. Loudoun's by-right process was the reason hyperscalers could land in Northern Virginia and be operational on a 24-month timeline. Special Exception in comparable jurisdictions adds 12-24 months and introduces meaningful cancellation risk. Some projects won't survive it. Others will, but with concessions — community-benefit agreements, water-use caps, height limits, façade requirements, traffic mitigation. Every one of those raises capex and reduces site-suitability ranking.

April 1: The court bit

Two weeks later, the Virginia Court of Appeals halted the "Digital Gateway" — Prince William County's mega-rezoning that would have permitted ~25M sq ft of new data-center development adjacent to the Manassas National Battlefield. The Court ruled that PWC's process violated public-hearing requirements. The rezoning is, for now, void.

This isn't merely about Digital Gateway. The same procedural argument is now being filed in two other Virginia counties with similar mega-projects in the pipeline. If even one of those succeeds, the entire Northern Virginia development pipeline through 2028 gets re-litigated — at exactly the moment Dominion's transmission build-out assumes that pipeline executes.

What Dominion is doing

Continuing to build. Dominion's most recent IRP (Integrated Resource Plan) filing with the Virginia State Corporation Commission projects 8-12 GW of new data-center load in Northern Virginia through 2032 and prices the transmission CapEx accordingly. Their public response to both the Loudoun ordinance and the Court ruling has been some version of "we serve load where load shows up; the local zoning question is for the counties." Which is, in the narrowest legal sense, true.

It is also functionally an admission that Dominion is taking the bet that Loudoun, PWC, and adjacent counties will not meaningfully constrain growth. That is a bet. Currently:

  • Loudoun: by-right gone, Special Exception required for every project.
  • Prince William: Digital Gateway frozen, Court appeal could reopen the entire 25M sq ft pipeline.
  • Fauquier: ordinance restricting DC siting near sensitive areas pending board vote.
  • Stafford: 12-month moratorium proposal entered the planning commission docket in March.
  • Spotsylvania: county supervisors publicly cooled on the K2 megasite after community backlash on water use.

Five Virginia counties moving against the build-out simultaneously is not a Loudoun story. It's a region-wide repricing of the local political tolerance for data-center load. If Dominion's IRP load forecast is wrong by even 30%, the transmission CapEx already permitted is meaningfully stranded. Ratepayers eat that — under Virginia's rate structure, residential and small-commercial customers backstop the transmission build whether the data centers materialize or not.

What's the right move?

Dominion has four real options:

  1. Stay the course. Build the transmission as planned, assume the legal challenges lose and the bills die in committee, and pass the cost through if they don't. This is the current plan. It's defensible only if you have private information that the build-out will continue — which Dominion may, given relationship depth with hyperscaler land teams.
  2. Pause non-critical T&D. Pull back on the northern segments, complete the southern reinforcements already under construction, and wait 12-18 months for political clarity. Reduces stranded-asset risk; cedes growth to other utilities if the build-out resumes.
  3. Reorient to Southside Virginia + central Virginia. Spotsylvania, Culpeper, Louisa, Mecklenburg — counties further from the Beltway with less existing development and less organized opposition. Build transmission to where the future DC pipeline can actually clear local approval.
  4. Engage with Loudoun on a revised framework. Negotiate a structured by-right return with concessions on water, noise, and height — the kind of deal Sterling, VA managed in 2023 with its solar overlay district. Slow but durable.

Our read: Dominion is doing #1 with a quiet ramp toward #3. Engineering studies for new transmission corridors into Southside have appeared in commission filings since November. But the pace of #3 is wrong for the velocity of opposition in #1. We think they're 6-12 months late.

Dominion is taking a $15 billion bet that Loudoun reverses course. The base rate of Loudoun reversing course is currently zero.

What investors should read from this

Three things. First: Dominion's stranded-asset risk on Northern Virginia transmission is materially higher than current sell-side models price. Second: the secondary effect is on hyperscaler REIT exposure. Digital Realty, Equinix, and comparable peers have meaningful Loudoun-cluster exposure both in operating assets and in the development pipeline. Special Exception delays compress the development NAV per square foot substantially. Third: the rate-base implications hit Virginia residential and small-commercial customers in 2027-2028 if the load doesn't materialize. That becomes a political problem for Dominion, which loops back into pressure to slow the build-out — which is the right move they should have taken voluntarily a year ago.

What we'll be tracking next

Three indicators week-over-week: Court of Appeals movement on the two follow-on Digital Gateway suits (next hearing window: late June), sponsor counts on the two Virginia state-level moratorium bills currently in committee (HB 1515 and a forthcoming SB version), and Loudoun's first three Special Exception hearings under the new process. The first of those is scheduled for April 28. If even one of the three pilot projects gets denied, the chilling effect on the development pipeline is immediate, and Dominion's IRP starts looking very different.

Ballot measure · April 25, 2026 · 8 min read

Ohio just made data-center bans a constitutional question.

By the editors

On April 18, Attorney General Dave Yost certified — and the Ohio Ballot Board unanimously approved — a proposed amendment to the Ohio Constitution that would ban future construction of large data centers. Petitioners are now collecting signatures. If they qualify, Ohio becomes the first US state to put data-center siting on the ballot as a constitutional question, not a statutory one. The difference is everything.

Most state-level data-center restrictions to date have been statutory: a bill passes the legislature, the governor signs (or doesn't), the law lives in the state code. Statutes can be repealed by a future legislature, narrowed by sympathetic AGs, carved up by exemption clauses negotiated quietly with industry. That's why six of the moratorium bills currently in committee across the country include sunset clauses — legislators want the political win without the durable constraint.

A constitutional amendment is a different animal. To overturn it, you need either another statewide referendum or a supermajority of the legislature in two consecutive sessions. It is, by design, very hard to undo. That's the point of the Ohio coalition's choice of vehicle.

Who's behind it

The lead petitioner is Portage Residents for Responsible Development, joined by a coalition that includes (per the Ballot Board filing) groups in Trumbull, Mahoning, Lake, and Stark counties, plus statewide ratepayer-advocacy organizations. The trigger event was AEP Ohio's pending rate case at PUCO — the Public Utilities Commission of Ohio — in which AEP is attempting to roll new transmission costs for hyperscaler load into the residential rate base. Portage and surrounding counties host enough proposed DC capacity that the rate-impact math has become locally personal in a way most other states haven't yet experienced.

Critically, this is not an environmental coalition. It is a ratepayer coalition with environmental endorsements. That framing is the lift — the same lift Maine's LD 307 supporters used to bring moderate-Republican co-sponsors on board. Anti-AI rhetoric does not move a Trumbull County voter; "AEP is going to put $40 on your monthly bill so Microsoft can build in Lordstown" moves a Trumbull County voter.

The procedural mechanics

AG Yost's certification means the petition language is legally clean — it doesn't violate single-subject or other constitutional requirements. The Ballot Board's 5-0 unanimous approval cleared it for circulation. To qualify for the November 2026 ballot, petitioners need to collect roughly 413,000 valid signatures from at least 44 of Ohio's 88 counties by early July. That's a meaningful but achievable number. The 2018 Ohio drug-policy amendment qualified with similar geographic distribution requirements.

If the measure makes the ballot, polling becomes the next question. We don't have a public poll on data-center sentiment in Ohio yet. We do have polling on adjacent questions — utility rate transparency (favorable supermajorities), restrictions on out-of-state corporate purchases of land (also favorable), environmental review requirements for industrial sites (favorable). Those are the rhyming questions, and they all poll well in Ohio. We'd be surprised if a clean ratepayer-framed DC ban polled below 55%.

Why this changes the national board

Three reasons. First: Ohio is the first state where the DC question becomes a 2026 election issue at the statewide level. Up to now the activity has been in legislative committees and county commissions, both essentially invisible to general-election voters. A November 2026 ballot question forces every Ohio statewide candidate — gubernatorial, AG, Senate — to take a public position. That manufactures political durability for the cause regardless of whether the amendment passes.

Second: it gives every other state's coalition a playbook. Constitutional amendment processes vary by state, but at least 18 states (CA, AZ, MO, MI, OK, OH, NE, ND, SD, OR, MS, CO, ID, MT, MA, IL, AR, ME) allow citizen-initiated constitutional amendments. Many have already-organized ratepayer coalitions. If Ohio qualifies and polls well, expect California and Michigan in 2026-2027.

Third: it raises the cost of capital for Ohio DC builds, immediately, before the vote. Project finance for a hyperscale DC assumes 30-year operating life. A ballot measure that has even a 30-40% probability of passing in seven months adds materially to discount rates. Hyperscaler site teams will already be reading this, and it will already be moving Ohio down their candidate list. Whether the amendment passes is almost secondary to whether it gets to the ballot.

The amendment doesn't need to pass to work. It just needs to be on the ballot when site teams are doing their 2027 land options.

What developers should read from this

Ohio is now a contingent-risk state, not an open-field state. If you have active land options in Ohio, you should be modeling a 30-40% probability that those options are exercise-blocked by the amendment. Pricing should reflect that. Better to negotiate option extensions and wait for the November outcome than to commit capital to engineering studies on sites that may be voided.

For comparison, Texas, North Carolina, Iowa, and Minnesota currently have no comparable ballot or amendment activity. The risk delta is real and it's pricing-relevant.

What investors should read from this

Two things. First: any AEP / FirstEnergy exposure with material Ohio rate-base assumptions tied to data-center load forecasts needs a haircut on probability of approved cost recovery. PUCO can read polls; commissioners are appointed but they're political. Second: the cascade is the bigger story. If Ohio qualifies and polls well, CA and MI start their own petitions within months, and the 2027-2028 hyperscale build pipeline starts to look very different in 4-6 large states simultaneously.

Statutory bans can be unwound. Constitutional bans cannot. That's why the activists picked this vehicle. That's why finance teams should read it differently.

What we'll be tracking next

Three weekly indicators: signature-collection cadence (petitioners typically publish weekly progress through their county chairs), AEP Ohio's rate-case status at PUCO (a favorable rate decision for AEP would energize the ballot coalition further), and whether any Ohio statewide candidate — particularly Republican — publicly endorses or opposes the amendment in the next 60 days. The first major endorsement will be the leading indicator of the November vote.

County-level cascade · April 25, 2026 · 10 min read

The Kansas belt: how three counties shut down their own pipeline in 90 days.

By the editors

Saline, Harvey, and Sedgwick counties don't show up in the national DC-opposition narrative. They should. Between January and April 2026 all three passed multi-year moratoriums on data-center construction in their unincorporated areas — none coordinating with the others, all unanimous, all under the political radar. The story isn't the moratoriums. It's how cleanly the playbook now travels.

Saline County moved first. On a quiet January meeting agenda, the Board of County Commissioners voted 4-0 to place a three-year moratorium on construction of data centers, nuclear power facilities, and hydrogen energy infrastructure in unincorporated Saline County. The bundling is the interesting part — not just data centers, but a category of what the local ordinance describes as "high-intensity industrial energy consumers." That language is being copied.

Harvey County followed roughly seven weeks later with a unanimous resolution placing its own moratorium through the end of 2028 — also in unincorporated areas, also broad enough to cover not just data centers but any high-load energy consumer. Sedgwick County extended what had been an initial 90-day pause into a 6-month freeze running through June 11, 2026, with a state lawmaker publicly asking the commissioners to extend the pause to three years.

That's three counties — covering Salina, Newton, and Wichita — pausing data-center development across roughly 4,500 square miles of central Kansas. None of these places were on any hyperscaler's published target list. That is exactly what makes the cascade interesting. The moratoriums are preemptive, not reactive. Local commissioners are now moving against a problem that hasn't arrived yet, on the assumption that it will.

The mechanism nobody is writing about

County commissioners talk to each other. There's no organized anti-DC advocacy network in Kansas — at least none that we can identify in public filings or social media activity. What there is, instead, is the Kansas Association of Counties (KAC), which holds quarterly meetings, runs a member listserv, and circulates model ordinances. Between October 2025 and January 2026, KAC's land-use working group circulated a one-page briefing memo on data-center moratoriums that included sample ordinance language. We've seen the memo. It cites the Loudoun County (VA) ordinance and the early Ohio moratoriums as precedent. The exact phrasing about "high-intensity industrial energy consumers" appears in the KAC memo and then verbatim in the Saline ordinance.

That is how this kind of policy now propagates. Not through national press, not through advocacy campaigns, but through sleepy professional associations that local government staff actually read because the materials are practical and the liability concerns are concrete. Once the model language is in circulation, the activation cost for county #2 and county #3 drops to near zero — they're not drafting an ordinance, they're approving the same one their peers approved.

Why this matters more than the Maine bill

Maine's LD 307 is a national story because it's a statewide bill that hyperscalers were genuinely targeting. Kansas's county cascade is, in absolute terms, less consequential — no hyperscaler had committed capex to Saline County. But as a signal of how the policy now moves, it may be more important.

State-level fights are visible and well-resourced. Industry lobbyists, governor's offices, statewide press all pay attention. County-level fights are invisible until the ordinance is already enacted. The political defense is built before any developer even knows there was an attack. Once the ordinance is in place, the development pipeline routes around the county — and once enough counties have one, the route-around starts to compress.

Compounding this: the KAC memo isn't a Kansas-only artifact. Equivalent associations exist in every state — National Association of Counties at the federal level, plus 50 state chapters. If KAC's land-use working group wrote a model ordinance, the working groups in every other state can read the Saline outcome and decide to circulate the same memo. That's the propagation channel that should worry anyone running site selection.

Where else this is already happening

Pull on the thread and the pattern shows up. Champaign County, Illinois — one-year moratorium, unanimous, January 2026. Manitowoc County, Wisconsin — one-year moratorium, unanimous, February 2026. Orange County, North Carolina — one-year moratorium, 6-0, March 2026. DeKalb County, Georgia — extended moratorium through June 2026, unanimous. Cumberland County, North Carolina — public hearing scheduled May 18 with three commissioners already publicly committed to a 1-3 year ban.

That's eight counties across six states, all in the past four months, all unanimous or near-unanimous, all in jurisdictions with no active hyperscaler project. This is no longer an opposition story. It is a default-mode story. The default planning posture for a US county in 2026 is increasingly: pause first, evaluate later.

When eight unrelated county boards in six states converge on the same procedural choice in 90 days, you are not watching coordinated opposition. You are watching the new default.

What developers should read from this

Three things. First: your candidate-list strategy needs to be county-down, not state-down. A friendly statewide environment (no moratorium bills in committee) tells you nothing about the receptiveness of the specific county you're optioning land in. Pull the county-level minutes, planning commission agendas, and county-association memos — not just the state-level filings.

Second: the early-warning indicator is the county association. KAC, ICCA (Iowa), MACo (Maryland), CSAC (California), and equivalent bodies have land-use working groups whose meeting agendas are public. If "data-center ordinances" appears on the agenda for the next quarterly meeting in a state where you have land options, that is the moment to engage — not after the model ordinance has already circulated.

Third: the moratorium structure now circulating bundles data centers with other high-load uses (hydrogen, nuclear, large crypto operations). That's a structural risk for any co-located energy strategy your team has been pursuing as a workaround. The same ordinance that blocks the data center also blocks the on-site SMR you were going to use to argue the project was self-supplied.

What activists and county commissioners should read from this

The model ordinance approach works. It's faster than statewide legislation, it's procedurally simpler, and it produces durable outcomes that survive the next election cycle. If you're a county commissioner reading this and your county hasn't yet had a data-center proposal, the right time to put a moratorium framework in place is before you have one. Saline, Harvey, and Sedgwick are the proof of concept. The model language is in your state county association's working group; ask for it.

What we'll be tracking next

Three indicators: which other Kansas counties take up the Saline-template ordinance in the May-June commission cycle (we're watching Reno, Butler, and Sumner), whether the Iowa State Association of Counties picks up a comparable memo (Iowa is the highest-leverage next state), and whether any of the eight existing county moratoriums get challenged in court. The first court ruling — affirmative or negative — will be the most consequential signal of how durable this propagation pattern actually is.
